Kaingang

The Kaingang are a people belonging to the Jê family who currently occupy 46 indigenous lands located in States of São Paulo, Paraná, Santa Catarina and Rio Grande do Sul and their population is estimated at 37,470 people As they are distributed across four states, the situation of the communities presents the most varied conditions. In all cases, however, its social structure and cosmological principles remain in force, always updated by the different circumstances they have been going through.

Cosmolology and mythology

The dispersion of Kaingang groups across the fields and forests of their traditional territory did not and does not prevent that these indigenous people recognize a common cosmological system. Indeed, even today kaingang groups, in addition to a record common mythology, they share beliefs and practices regarding their ritual experiences – deep respect for the dead and attachment to the lands where their navels are buried indisputable expressions of the structuring value of cosmology for these Indians.

Origin Myth

There are few studies that are dedicated exclusively to the analysis of kaingang myths. There are, however, references recurring to myths collected by Borba (1882), Nimuendajú (1913) and Schaden (1956). We owe the first record of Kaingang mythology to Telêmaco Borba, who published, in 1882, the myth of the people's origin kaingang and the myth of the origin of corn. The first tells the story of the mythological brothers Kamé and Kairu who, after the great flood, left the interior of the Crinjijimbé mountain range. “In times gone by, there was a great flood that submerged the entire land inhabited by our ancestors. Only the summit of the Crinjijimbé mountain range emerged from the waters.. The Caingangues, Cayrucrés and Camés swam towards it carrying burning logs of firewood in their mouths. The Cayrucrés and the Camés, tired, drowned; their souls went to live in the center of the mountain...” After the waters dried up, the Caingangues settled in the vicinity of Crinjijimbé. The Cayrucrés and Camés, whose souls had gone to live in the center from the mountains, they started to make his way inside her; after a lot of work they ended up exiting through two paths” (Borba 1908:20-21).

The division into halves of Kamé and Kairu, the red thread to which Nimuendajú refers, appears in the origin myth through the trajectory of the mythological brothers Kamé and Kairu. It is these cultural heroes who give the kaingang moieties their name, they are the ones who, in the course of the myth, created the beings of nature. “Kanyerú made snakes, Kamé, jaguars. He first made a jaguar and painted it, then Kanyerú made a deer. Kamé said to the jaguar: ‘Eat the deer, but don’t eat us’. Then he made a tapir, ordering it to eat people and animals. The tapir, however, did not understand the order. Kamé repeated it twice in vain; then he said to him, angrily: ‘You’re going to eat nettle leaves, you’re no good!’. Kanyeru made snakes and ordered them to bite men and animals” (Nimuendajú 1986:87). The dualism expressed in the kaingang origin myth, as analyzed in light of Nimuendajú's contribution, presents two fundamental classificatory properties. Firstly, Kamé and Kairu dualism offers a comprehensive, totalizing system of classification – beings in nature, including men, bear the mark of halves and carry values ​​associated with them, such as: strong/weak, high/low, momentum/persistence. Secondly, kaingang dualism, in its mythological record, offers a formula for social organization through the establishment of rules of descent and marriage.

Marriage and descendants

Os irmãos mitológicos Kamé e Kairu não apenas criaram os seres da natureza, mas também as regras de conduta para os homens, definindo a fórmula de recrutamento das metades (patrilinearidade) e estabelecendo a forma como as metades deveriam relacionar-se (exogamia). “Chegaram a um campo grande, reuniram-se aos Kaingang e deliberaram casar os moços e as moças. Casaram-se primeiro os Kairucrés com as filhas dos Kamés, estes com as daqueles, e como ainda sobravam homens, casaram-se com as filhas dos Kaingang” (Borba 1908:22). Segundo a tradição kaingang os casamentos devem ser realizados entre indivíduos de metades opostas; os Kamé devem casar-se com os Kairu e vice-versa. Se os casamentos, idealmente, unem membros das metades Kamé e Kairu. Os filhos desse casamento ideal recebem a filiação da metade paterna. Diversos registros etnológicos revelam a ocorrência da descendência patrilinear. Teschauer (1927), por exemplo, afirma que segundo a tradição kaingang “a criança devia sua existência exclusivamente ao pai. A mãe era somente a depositária e guarda da prole (...) a condição do pai passava aos filhos e não a da mãe”(Teschauer 1927:44). Apesar do padrão patrilinear de descendência, a forma tradicional de residência entre os Kaingang é a matrilocalidade - após o casamento o genro passa a morar na casa do sogro. Este padrão de residência é comum a todos os grupos Jê. Como vimos, o pertencimento a uma metade decorre da descendência paterna. A ratificação desta identidade ocorre com a escolha de um nome para o recém-nascido. A nominação entre os Jê, e especificamente entre os Kaingang, tem sido descrita como um importante processo para o estabelecimento de identidades sociais. Os próprios heróis mitológicos Kamé e Kairu criaram e nomearam os seres da natureza. Os nomes pertencem às metades. As crianças, ao serem nomeadas, recebem sua identidade social, a qual, ao lado da descendência paterna, será sua marca distintiva. Ao contrário da descendência, que não pode ser alterada, os nomes podem ser manipulados a fim proteger a criança contra doenças ou outros infortúnios.

Impact of Christianization

The dualism expressed in the kaingang origin myth, as analyzed in light of Nimuendajú's contribution, presents two fundamental classificatory properties. Firstly, Kamé and Kairu dualism offers a system of comprehensive, totalizing classification – the beings of nature, including men, have the mark of halves and carry values ​​associated with them, such as: strong/weak, high/low, impetus/persistence. Secondly, kaingang dualism, in its mythological record, it offers a formula for social organization through the establishment of rules of descent and marriage. Both in the version of the origin myth collected by Borba and in that collected by Nimuendajú, the complementarity between the mythological brothers Kamé and Kairu is explicit: the Kamé worked during the day to make the animals that belong to this half, the Kairu, conversely, worked at night; the sun belongs to half Kamé, the moon to half Kairu. However, the complementarity is effectively expressed in the episodes of the origin myth, there are moments in this narrative that point to asymmetry, for a hierarchical relationship between the halves. Firstly, Kamé was the first to leave the interior of the land after the flood – this is an important characteristic for the unfolding of the ritual experience, as we will see later.
