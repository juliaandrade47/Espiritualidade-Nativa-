Pataxó

Pataxó is a language from the Macro-Jê trunk and the Maxakalí linguistic family. The Pataxó Indigenous people are practitioners of shamanism and Christianity. In 2010, 11,436 inhabitants (5,839 men and 5,597 women) were registered by SIASI (Indigenous Health Care Information System) and FUNASA (National Health Foundation) distributed in several villages, in the extreme south of the State of Bahia in 36 villages distributed across six Indigenous Lands, and in the state of Minas Gerais the Pataxó live in seven communities.
Aldeia Velha, tribe of Pataxós Indians in Arraial D’Ajuda, district of Porto Seguro, Bahia.

Ritual practices and festivals

The Pataxó very often refer to the Abatirás, “Baquirás” or “Abaquirás” and the Habiá. The first are related to Juacema or Ponta de Juacema, as this place is better known, a stretch of the coast of Porto Seguro where the cliffs extend over the sea, located between the Frade and Caraíva rivers, closer to the latter. Both the Bakirá and the Habiá are living beings, the latter being described by those who see them and communicate with them, as dark-skinned, speakers of the common language, of normal size and human appearance, and with the peculiarity of not eating salt. There is also the somsim saperé, a man with one leg wrapped around the other, full of wounds. It is human and invisible. But, just like humans, “wildlife animals” are enchanting. Caipora, for example, is a woman, owner of bush farming. It's a secret... because it's enchanted. It is widely said that in Pé do Monte “there are man-animals, they stay under the ground”. The boitatá is also an invisible man, with fire on his head. The gibburinha is also invisible, a small man who, from the trail, is a few centimeters long. But he likes women and gets them pregnant. In Barra Velha, four female gibura were born. He gnaws on mangaba, caxandó, guaru, the woman eats it... when the boy doesn't come out small, he comes out with teeth, he's a product of the gibura. He also has a water-fool quality, an enchanted water-folk, when he senses a woman, she dives deep with him and has sex. The skin is dark, like that of an otter or otter.

Awê ritual

The Awê ritual is the only one considered “an ancient thing”. It is “something that has always existed and that not even grandparents Some of the old people knew how to say when it started [...]. It seems that when an Awê was made in the past it was a single music/dance all the time. But doing an Awê is an expression that today [refers] to different party contexts [...] it encompasses a very varied set of choreographies, each with a specific meaning” (Grunewald, 1999, p. 251) . Awê requires cauim and, eventually, aluá, a fermented drink made from ground corn grains or fruit peels, such as pineapple, among others.

Awê ritual held at the Indigenous Shopping Center in Aldeia de Coroa Vermelha. Photo: Sarah Siqueira de Miranda, 2006.

Parties

Arsgwaksá

In August, Arsgwaksá is celebrated annually, the celebration of the anniversary of the Jaqueira Project, when, simultaneously, Pataxó culture is conveyed. The festivities include presentation of Awê, physical tests such as log races and different modalities of “public representation of Pataxó Indianness”, such as the traditional weddings, preceded by a demonstration of physical strength by the male suitors, i.e., the transport of wooden logs, the same ones used in competitions in indigenous games (Neves, 20123, p. 166-167). The so-called Holy Week was referred to as an occasion for the use of gourd masks, each under a different denomination. “There was a guy called Mandu, an animal with a big head, caipora, ox... He came out during Holy Week”. It is, even today, a period during which more formal habits are manifested, such as treating the elderly with a certain reverence, such as taking their blessings, kneeling, as an expression of the effective or presumptive kinship that unites the young to the oldest: “Blessings, my uncle! My cousin! My godfather!". Apparently, there is no direct and conscious connection, for the Pataxó, between Holy Week – a Christian festival commemorating the resurrection of Christ – and the use of animal masks. There would, most likely, only be the memory of a temporal marker of Christian influence.
